As Bitcoin‚Äôs price rose over 2017, so did its transaction fees. Bitcoin Core developers are any against block-size increase and are instead promising the Lightning network as a solution. However, the Lightning network is still in beta on the main net and there are open questions on its usability and adoption rate. This has left the Bitcoin network in a crippled state for several months on end.

This Bitcoin fee fiasco has generated significant interest in other cryptocurrencies with low fees and fast transaction times. RaiBlocks with its claim to near instant and free transactions with unlimited throughput capacity its this mold well. Starting in December, Bitcoin‚Äôs market cap dominance dropped from 60% to 33%, while more performant altcoins like RaiBlocks saw tremendous price increases. RaiBlocks was one of the biggest winners with price skyrocketing from $0.20 in December to almost $35 at its peak. It‚Äôs currently stabilizing at around $22.

Raiblock‚Äôs claim on its incredible speed and scalability has raised many people‚Äôs eyebrows since the best property of cryptocurrencies, decentralization, has always meant shitty scalability. Coupled with an almost miraculous rise in price, many people have started to question RaiBlocks‚Äôs legitimacy.

Let‚Äôs dive into RaiBlocks to see what it is, how it works, and whether it‚Äôs actually decentralized.

10,000 feet summary

  • Started in 2014. The main net was launched in 2017 and it‚Äôs fully functional.
  • Fixed, maximum supply of 133,248,289 RaiBlocks. All RaiBlocks has been released.
  • 0 transaction fee, unlimited throughput capacity, > 5 second confirmation time.
  • Uses a block-lattice data structure instead of the blockchain.
  • Consensus is maintained through a voting mechanism. Validator nodes vote on a world state they believe is true and the number of votes they have consists of the number of RaiBlocks the node owns and the number of RaiBlocks delegated to the node by other accounts.
  • The RaiBlocks team didn‚Äôt conduct an ICO and instead distributed RaiBlocks for free through a web faucet. 7,000,000 RaiBlocks were retained as developer funds.

Is RaiBlocks a scam?

I‚Äôve scoured through the whitepaper and looked over the team‚Äôs history and believe that RaiBlocks is everything it claims, with a few caveats. There are many open questions around its consensus algorithm. It‚Äôs not well tested and to my understanding, lacks peer review. This increases the risk of a critical bug that could take down the whole network. Although RaiBlocks in its current state does indeed support near instant and free transactions with unlimited throughput capacity, the jury is still out on whether the consensus algorithm is correct and robust.

RaiBlocks is not a scam.

How does RaiBlocks work?

Unlike most cryptocurrencies that use a traditional blockchain datastructure, RaiBlocks uses a novel data structure known as a block-lattice. While the blockchain consists of a singular global chain of blocks, every RaiBlocks account has its own blockchain.

The only account that can publish a transaction to an account‚Äôs blockchain is the account itself.

To send funds, the sender needs to issue a ‚Äúsend‚Äù transaction in its own blockchain and the receiver needs to issue a corresponding ‚Äúreceive‚Äù transaction, also in its own blockchain. This is why RaiBlocks is so fast. An account can very quickly update its own blockchain since its the only entity that can change it. These transactions are propagated across the network and as long as no validator discovers a conflict, the transaction is, for all intents and purposes, considered confirmed.

The RaiBlocks network consists of wallets and validator nodes. In order for a wallet to issue a transaction, it must send it to a validator node. The node validates the transaction and then passes to all other validator nodes its connected to. Every subsequent validator node performs the same validate-and-relay process until the whole network has seen the transaction.

A validator node keeps track of the state of the entire network by storing the blockchains of every single RaiBlocks address. Conflicts or ‚Äúforks‚Äù happen when a published transaction conflicts with a validator node‚Äôs internal state. To resolve the conflict, the node initiates a voting process by announcing the conflict to other nodes. The voting process occurs over a predefined period where each node votes on a state of the network it believes is correct. The number of votes a node has equals the amount of RaiBlocks it owns plus the RaiBlocks delegated to it from other accounts. When a state has more than 133,248,289/2 votes (133,248,289 is the total number of XRBs in existence), it‚Äôs automatically accepted as the real state of the network.

The RaiBlocks network provides no rewards for running a validator.

To mitigate spam, an account needs to perform easy proof of work to add a transaction to its own blockchain. This is, of course, not a cure all as transactions can still be created within a few seconds.

Team and history

The key developer behind RaiBlocks is Colin LeMahieu. LeMahieu started developing RaiBlocks in 2014 and the network was released in 2017. RaiBlocks didn‚Äôt hold an ICO and instead, distributed the cryptocurrency through a faucet on a website. Users need only complete a captcha in order to receive RaiBlocks.

The team behind RaiBlocks is small and fairly unknown but with the cryptocurrency‚Äôs recent success, LeMahieu is in the process of building a larger team. Look to RaiBlocks to make big ecosystem plays in 2018.

RaiBlocks vs IOTA

IOTA is another cryptocurrency that offers near instant and free transactions with high scalability. IOTA, like RaiBlocks, saw a tremendous increase in price over the past month. It‚Äôs not surprising that the two are frequently compared against each other. Here‚Äôs how the two cryptocurrencies differ.

As mentioned above, RaiBlocks uses a block-lattice structure where every account has its own blockchain. IOTA uses a global DAG data structure (called Tangle) where every node in the graph represents one whole transaction. In order to issue a new transaction, an account needs to validate two previous transactions through proof of work. The new transaction would then ‚Äúreference‚Äù or connect to the two previous transactions in the Tangle.

Whereas IOTA transactions happen in a single phase, i.e. one transaction that represents both a send and a receive, RaiBlocks transactions happen in two phases: a sender needs to issue a ‚Äúsend‚Äù transaction in its blockchain and the receiver needs to issue a corresponding ‚Äúreceive‚Äù transaction.

One major advantage RaiBlocks has over IOTA is in decentralization. Currently, IOTA needs a centralized Coordinator to ensure network security and direct network progress. This is because IOTA‚Äôs network security is directly tied to the amount of transactions occuring in the network. In low transaction conditions, transactions take longer to confirm and the network becomes more vulnerable to attacks. In theory, the Coordinator will eventually become unnecessary as the network reaches a critical mass of adoption but IOTA is far from this reality. A scary consequence of the existence of a Coordinator is the fact that the IOTA developers can freeze user funds at a whim‚Ä¶ and they have done so before (source).

The security of RaiBlocks‚Äôs network, on the other hand, does not depend on the amount of transactions going through it. As such, RaiBlocks currently doesn‚Äôt need a centralized entity as a crutch to be secure.

But it‚Äôs not all that bad for IOTA. Afterall, it was one the first movers for free and fast cryptocurrencies and it has an ecosystem advantage over RaiBlocks. IOTA has more users and the IOTA Foundation has more business connections. For example, the Foundation recently launched an IOTA Data Marketplace initiative with many large companies participating.

Weaknesses

Questions around the consensus algorithm

As mentioned above, there are many open questions around the consensus algorithm. Given that RaiBlocks is so young, it‚Äôs not well tested and lacks peer review. This introduces the risk of a serious flaw in the consensus algorithm. For example, what happens if there are not enough votes for a quorum in order to resolve a network conflict? Currently, validator nodes rely on the maximum supply number (133,248,289 RaiBlocks in existence) to determine a quorum and it‚Äôs totally possible that a voting process could not have enough validator nodes participating.

Here‚Äôs another big question I have: in the event that parts of the RaiBlocks network get separated for a long period of time (perhaps due to network issues or other freak occurences), what happens when you rejoin the separated networks? Will the rejoined network be crippled from all the voting processes that would inevitably take place? Is there a mathematical guarantee that the rejoined network will recover?

These are all very important questions and their answers will determine the robustness and, ultimately, the longevity of the RaiBlocks network.

Spam vulnerability

RaiBlocks, being inherently feeless, is highly vulnerable to spam. I could run a script where multiple accounts will constantly send transactions between each other creating massive blockchains. Run long enough, it will make validators incredibly expensive to maintain.

It costs almost nothing to create spam in the system yet spam can be very costly. Unlike the traditional blockchain where storage and computational costs are predicatable, RaiBlocks‚Äôs feeless transactions and scalability makes both costs highly unpredictable and there is no rule preventing them from skyrocketing.

Expect these spam attacks to occur as RaiBlocks increases in popularity and we will have to see how the development team responds.

RaiBlocks doesn‚Äôt incentivize decentralization

RaiBlocks validator nodes are quite expensive to run with high network throughput costs and modest processing and storage costs. Since there‚Äôs no reward for running a RaiBlocks validator node, validator node operators are prone to centralization.

Some people argue that businesses relying on the RaiBlocks network will be incentivized to run validator nodes to secure the network. Large investors might also be incentivized to run validator nodes. However, we have yet to see if this is true.

Limited applications - no smart contracts

Smart contracts are a very powerful extension to cryptocurrencies, one that I don‚Äôt think many people have even begun to appreciate. I have a strong feeling that any future winning cryptocurrencies must support smart contracts. However, I doubts on whether RaiBlocks can support smart contracts given that it‚Äôs system is so radically different.

Bottomline - am I bullish on RaiBlocks?

RaiBlocks works as described - near instant and feeless transactions with unlimited transaction throughput. It has a lot of potential to be a serious challenge to big names like IOTA and even Bitcoin. However, RaiBlocks is currently being held back by an unproven consensus algorithm, spam vulnerability, and the lack of potential for smart contracts.

Nevertheless, I‚Äôm quite bullish.
